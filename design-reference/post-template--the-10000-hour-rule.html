The 10,000-Hour Rule, Revisited

How long mastery really takes — and what the famous rule gets wrong about the people who reach it.

Hami Tahm
Dec 2025 · 7 min read

Most people have heard the claim: it takes 10,000 hours to master anything. It's a clean, quotable number — and like most clean, quotable numbers, it hides more than it reveals.

The figure comes from research on expert performance, later popularized into a rule of thumb. But somewhere between the study and the bestseller, an important nuance got lost. The number was never meant to be a finish line you cross. It was an average — and averages tell you very little about any individual path.

Where the number comes from

The original work studied elite performers in fields with clear feedback and structured practice. What mattered wasn't just time logged, but the quality of that time: deliberate, focused, uncomfortable practice aimed squarely at weaknesses.

Ten thousand hours of doing the same thing comfortably is not ten thousand hours of getting better.

This is the part the rule erases. Two people can each spend 10,000 hours and end up in completely different places, because one was practicing and the other was merely repeating.

Time is the denominator. Deliberate effort is the numerator. The rule only mentions the denominator.

What this means in practice

If you're trying to get good at something, the question isn't "how many hours do I need?" It's:

  • Am I working at the edge of my current ability, or staying comfortable?
  • Do I get fast, honest feedback on whether I'm improving?
  • Am I isolating and attacking specific weaknesses?

Answer yes to those, and you'll compress the timeline dramatically. Answer no, and 10,000 hours may not be enough.

The founder parallel

I think about this constantly in building companies. You can run a venture for years and learn almost nothing, or run a brutal six-month experiment and learn more than a decade of comfortable operating would teach. The discipline is the same one I wrote about here: it's not the time, it's what you do inside it.

So revisit the rule, but don't worship it. Mastery isn't a number you reach. It's a quality of attention you sustain.
